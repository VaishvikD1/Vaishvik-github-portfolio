Personal Website Project Color Research

Color, Mood & Personality

An exploration of how color combinations shape emotion, perception, and identity in web design.

Research Questions

1. What are "warm" and "cool" colors, and what feelings do they evoke?

Colors are traditionally divided into two broad temperature families based on their position on the color wheel. Warm colors reds, oranges, yellows, and warm browns sit on the side of the wheel associated with fire and sunlight. They are called "warm" not because they literally raise temperature, but because they trigger psychological associations with heat, energy, and proximity. Studies in environmental psychology show that warm-colored rooms are consistently rated as feeling warmer, more stimulating, and more sociable than equivalent cool-colored spaces.

Cool colors blues, blue-greens, and most purples occupy the opposite arc of the wheel, evoking sky, water, and shadow. They tend to feel more distant, calmer, and more intellectual. A cool palette can lower perceived room temperature by several degrees and slow perceived time, which is why hospitals, spas, and tech companies often default to blue: it reads as trustworthy, precise, and unhurried.

It's worth noting that temperature is not absolute. A red shifted toward purple cools down; a green shifted toward yellow warms up. The perceived temperature of any single hue also shifts depending on what surrounds it which leads directly to the second question.

2. How does a color's effect change depending on the colors nearby? (Simultaneous Contrast)

Simultaneous contrast, first described systematically by Michel Eugène Chevreul in 1839 and later demonstrated by Josef Albers in his Interaction of Color (1963), is the phenomenon by which colors alter each other's perceived hue, value, and saturation when placed side by side. No color exists in isolation every color is partly defined by what surrounds it.

Classic example: Place the same middle gray square on a white background and on a black background. The gray on white appears darker; the gray on black appears lighter. The gray hasn't changed the eye has, because it subtracts the ambient light level from every local color judgment.

For web design this has several practical consequences. A muted, desaturated accent color can appear vibrant when surrounded by neutrals, but washed-out when placed next to a saturated color. A CTA button that looks great in a mockup can disappear on a final page if the surrounding hues compete with it. Warm backgrounds make cool elements appear cooler (and vice versa), which designers exploit to create temperature contrast that guides the eye. Text legibility is also affected: a dark green on a light green background may technically pass contrast ratios but still feel harder to read because the hues are too similar.

3. Cultural differences in color meaning

Color symbolism is not universal it is socially constructed and varies significantly across cultures, historical periods, and contexts. Three prominent examples:

White: In most Western cultures, white signifies purity, weddings, and new beginnings. Across much of East Asia China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam white is the color of mourning and funerals. A healthcare website designed primarily for a Western audience with a white palette would carry a very different emotional charge if deployed unchanged for a Chinese-speaking audience.

Green: In the United States and Europe, green strongly connotes nature, sustainability, and financial prosperity ("greenbacks"). In some Islamic traditions, green is a sacred color associated with paradise and the Prophet Muhammad, lending it a spiritual gravitas absent in Western branding contexts. In China, a man wearing a green hat is associated with marital infidelity a detail that has derailed more than one international product launch.

Red: Red means danger or urgency in most Western design systems (error states, stop signs). In China and much of South and East Asia, red is the color of luck, prosperity, and celebration which is why it dominates Chinese New Year imagery and wedding traditions. In South Africa, red is associated with mourning, while in the Middle East it can connote caution or danger much as in the West.

The broader lesson for web design is that global or multilingual sites must audit palette choices against the cultural context of each audience segment rather than assuming a single palette will land consistently worldwide.

Research Sources

  • Albers, Josef. Interaction of Color. Yale University Press, 1963 (50th anniversary ed. 2013). The foundational text on simultaneous contrast, afterimage, and color relativity, demonstrated through hands-on plate exercises. The source of the gray-on-different-grounds example above.
  • Elliot, A. J., & Maier, M. A. (2014). "Color Psychology: Effects of Perceiving Color on Psychological Functioning in Humans." Annual Review of Psychology, 65, 95–120. A peer-reviewed meta-analysis summarizing decades of research on how color temperature, saturation, and hue affect emotion, cognition, and behavior across cultures. annualreviews.org →
  • Heller, Eva. Wie Farben wirken: Farbpsychologie, Farbsymbolik, kreative Farbgestaltung. Rowohlt, 1989. (Translated editions widely available.) A large-scale survey of color associations across European cultures, covering emotional, symbolic, and cross-cultural dimensions of each major hue.
  • Smashing Magazine. "Color Theory for Designers, Part 1–3." Smashing Magazine, 2010. (Cameron Chapman) A widely-cited web-design-specific three-part series covering warm/cool, simultaneous contrast, cultural context, and how to build palettes for digital products. smashingmagazine.com →
  • Adobe Color. Color wheel and palette extraction tool. Adobe Inc. Used to extract and adjust the color palettes from each reference image in Part 3 of this report. color.adobe.com →
